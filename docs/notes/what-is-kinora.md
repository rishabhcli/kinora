# What is Kinora?

*A plain-English explainer. No tech background needed.*

> **In one sentence:** Kinora turns any book into a movie you watch *while you read it* — and the movie is created on the spot, scene by scene, just ahead of wherever your eyes are on the page.

---

## Picture it

Imagine opening a book on your screen. On the **left** is the book, exactly as it was written. On the **right**, a movie of the story plays.

As you read, a narrator reads the words aloud, the words light up one by one — like karaoke — and the page turns by itself to keep pace with you. You can just watch, just read, or do both at once.

Here's the surprising part: **nobody filmed that movie ahead of time.** It's being created *as you read*, a few seconds in front of you — like a film crew sketching the next scene the moment before you walk into it. Keep reading and it keeps drawing. Skip ahead and it catches up. Put the book down and it quietly waits.

That's the whole idea: **you don't read the book — you watch it.**

---

## What it's actually like to use

**1. You add a book.** Upload a PDF or pick a classic. Kinora quietly reads the whole thing first — figuring out who the characters are, what they look like, where scenes take place. (This part is fast and happens before you start.)

**2. You start reading.** The movie begins playing alongside the text. A voice narrates, the words highlight in time with the narration, and the page flips itself to follow along.

**3. You set the pace.** Because the movie is built around *your* reading speed, it's always ready right where you are. Read slowly and it relaxes. Read quickly and it speeds up to meet you. There's no waiting around staring at a loading spinner.

**4. You can take over as the director.** Don't like something? Point at any part of the screen and say it in plain words — *"make her coat red,"* *"this scene is too fast,"* *"wrong room."* The movie updates to match, and it remembers your taste for next time.

---

## Why this is special (and not just a gimmick)

Two things make Kinora different from "type a prompt, get a video" tools:

**It stays consistent.** The hardest part of turning a long story into video is that AI tends to "forget" — a character's face changes halfway through, colors drift, objects appear and vanish. Kinora keeps a kind of **story memory**: a running record of what every character looks like, sounds like, and what has already happened. Every new scene is checked against that memory, so the heroine looks like the heroine from chapter 1 to chapter 20. The movie holds together instead of falling apart.

**It only makes what you'll actually see.** A whole book would be hours of video, and most of it would never be watched. So Kinora never makes the entire film — it makes just the next little bit, right before you get there, and saves anything you've already seen so re-reading is instant. It's efficient by design, the way a smart assistant doesn't do work you didn't ask for.

---

## Who it's for

Kinora is **reading-alongside, not reading-replacing.** The real book stays on screen the whole time — the video is there to pull you through it. That makes it genuinely useful for:

- **Reluctant readers and people with ADHD** — the movie keeps you moving forward; the highlighted words keep you actually reading, not just watching a cartoon.
- **People with dyslexia** — hearing the words while seeing them highlighted is a proven aid to reading.
- **Language learners** — watch the scene, hear the line, see the word, all at a comfortable pace.
- **Comic, manga, and indie creators** — turn static pages and panels into instant animated versions.

The bigger idea: it uses the format that's *shrinking* our attention spans — short, auto-playing, scrolling video — to give us back the one thing those attention spans struggle with: **books.**

---

## What it is *not*

- It's **not** a replacement for reading — the words stay front and center.
- It's **not** a pre-made video library — every story becomes a movie on demand, shaped around how *you* read it.
- It's **not** a single magic "make a video" button — it's more like a small film crew (a writer, a director, a continuity checker, a quality reviewer) working together behind the scenes to keep the story looking right.

---

## About the name

A **Kinora** was a real device from the early 1900s: you'd turn a little crank and a reel of still photographs would spin fast enough to look like motion — *a book you could watch.* That's exactly the feeling this app is going for, just over a hundred years later.
